Part II - Key Insights from Prosper's Loan Data

by Aly Mobarak

Dataset Overview

This data set contains 113,937 loans with 81 variables on each loan, including loan amount, borrower rate (or interest rate), current loan status, borrower income, and many others. It comes from Prosper, an institution that introduced U.S. consumers to an innovative new approach to personal finance called peer-to-peer lending. Almost twenty years later, Prosper has helped over 1.7 million customers achieve financial well-being through a comprehensive suite of products.

The Relationship Between Prosper Score and Loan Outcomes

The Prosper Score is a risk assessment score ranging from 1-11, with 1 being most risky, and 11 most trustworthy.

The purpose of this plot is to visualize the relationship between Prosper Score (Prosper's creditworthiness rating) and the outcome of loans ("Contract In Tact, Success" vs. "Breached, Payment Failed"). This plot can help to determine:

  • If higher Prosper Scores (which suggest a lower risk) correlate with a higher success rate in loan repayments.
  • Conversely, if lower Prosper Scores (indicating higher risk) show a greater likelihood of loan repayment failure.

Observations: Prosper's portfolio is looking great, as across all risk levels, most loans are completed, with varying percentages. Generally, as the rating increases, the likelihood of completed loans increases and the failed likelihood decreases. Moreover, "Breached" statuses appear less frequently as the Prosper Score increases, which supports the idea that the Prosper Score is an effective risk indicator. Overall, the chart suggests that the Prosper Score is a reliable predictor of loan outcomes, as higher scores appear to correlate with more favorable loan statuses.

Loan Amount, Term, and Monthly Payment Dynamics

Now that we have examined the Prosper Score's predictive strength on loan performance, let's shift our focus to the practical implications of loan terms. Our next analysis layer reveals how the duration of a loan (short, medium, or long-term) affects the borrower's monthly payment obligations. Let's delve into this relationship and uncover the strategic decisions behind choosing the right loan term.

This plot aims to illustrate the relationship between the Loan Original Amount and the Monthly Loan Payment, with the color-coding indicating the term length of the loan (12, 36, or 60 months). This plot is particularly useful for assessing loan affordability and identifying patterns in loan structuring across different term lengths. It provides a visual tool for lenders to assess risk and for borrowers to understand their potential monthly obligations relative to the loan amount and term they're considering.

There's a general increase in monthly payments as the loan amount gets higher, which is basic financial knowledge. The loan term would influence the monthly payments of course. The 12 months loan would have to be repaid over a low number of months, increasing the number of monthly payments! This suggests that the financial impact of the loan term length on the monthly payment is significant. The steeper slope for the 12-month term implies that for any given loan amount, the monthly payments are substantially higher when the loan is to be repaid over a shorter period. On the other hand, the gentler slopes for the 36- and 60-month terms indicate more affordable monthly payments for the same amount of loan, due to the extended repayment period. This plot reinforces the concept that while the loan amount is a primary driver of the monthly payment, other factors like term and interest rates significantly influence the repayment schedule. It can be very informative for borrowers considering the implications of different loan terms and for lenders when assessing the potential for repayment defaults across varying loan terms.

Distribution of Borrower APR Across All Loans

Now, let me finish up the full circle by bringing the focus back to the borrowers perspective, highlighting what theyre paying on their loans and how this interacts with their creditworthiness and loan performance.

The APRs vary widely, with a notable peak around 0.35, indicating that this rate is quite common among loans. The distribution suggests that while there is a range of interest rates applied, there is a clustering around certain APRs that are likely linked to specific borrower risk profiles. The distribution is somewhat left-skewed, with the bulk of the loans having APRs between approximately 0.1 and 0.35. There are fewer loans with very low APRs (below 0.1), which might be offered to borrowers with excellent credit profiles. There is a small peak near 0.2, indicating another common APR level. Loans with APRs greater than 0.35 are less common, which might be due to higher rates being less attractive or available only to borrowers with riskier credit profiles. The histogram reveals that while there's a broad spread of APRs, there are specific APRs that are much more commonly assigned to loans, likely correlating with common borrower credit categories and loan terms.
